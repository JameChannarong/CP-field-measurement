A reference electrode provides a stable, reproducible electrochemical potential used as the measurement baseline for structure-to-electrolyte potential readings in cathodic protection systems.

Survey Methods

Two complementary field surveys verify CP effectiveness along the full pipeline: CIPS maps the pipe-to-soil potential at close intervals, while DCVG pinpoints coating defects by detecting surface voltage gradients.

CIPS — Potential Profiling DCVG — Holiday Detection Used Together for Full Assessment

🚶 Close Interval Potential Survey (CIPS)

A technician walks the pipeline route, dragging a reference electrode along the soil surface every 1–3 m, recording the pipe-to-soil potential at each point. A current interrupter cycles the CP system ON/OFF to capture both readings at every location.

📐 Effect of Anode Location on Measurement

Measurements taken near an anode show more negative ON potentials due to the larger IR drop in the current path. The OFF (instant-off) potential is not affected by IR drop and always reflects the true polarised potential of the pipe.

💡
Key rule: Always use the instant-off (OFF) potential to assess the protection level — it excludes IR drop and is the true polarised potential at the pipe surface.

Key Takeaways

2

3
Both surveys are used together: CIPS identifies underprotected zones; DCVG locates the coating defects responsible.
4
Always use the OFF (instant-off) potential for protection assessment — eliminates the IR drop component from the reading.

IR Drop

IR drop (ohmic drop) is the voltage error introduced into a CP potential measurement by current flowing through the soil resistance between the pipe and the reference electrode. It causes the measured ON potential to appear more negative than the pipe's true protection level.

VON = VOFF − IR Error hides underprotection Instant-off technique eliminates it

⚙️ How IR Drop Occurs

When CP current (I) flows through the soil resistance (R) between the reference electrode and the pipe, it creates a voltage drop V = I × R. This adds to the measured potential, making it appear more negative than the pipe's true polarised potential.

How to Perform the Instant-Off Measurement

1
Install a current interrupter on the rectifier output. Set cycle: e.g. 4 s ON / 1 s OFF.
2
Synchronise the voltmeter to the interrupter cycle so it reads immediately after current switches off.
3
Record the instant-off potential (first reading after switch-off, before depolarisation begins).
4
Compare VOFF to the −850 mV criterion. This value is free of IR drop and represents the true protection level.

Key Takeaways

1
IR drop makes VON appear more negative than the pipe's true potential — it can mask an underprotected pipe.
2
The magnitude of IR drop = I × Rsoil. High soil resistance or high current output = larger error.
3
The instant-off technique (interrupting current and reading immediately) eliminates IR drop from the measurement.
4
Always assess protection using VOFF (instant-off). Using VON alone risks misjudging protection status.

Current Measurement

Measure CP current using shunts, clamp ammeters, and 2-wire or 4-wire test stations. Understand Ohm's Law in action and determine both the magnitude and direction of current flow.

Shunt Calculator 2-Wire Method 4-Wire Method Current Direction

Measurement Methods

🔢
Multimeter / Ammeter
Direct reading inserted in series with the circuit. The circuit must be opened to insert the meter in-line.
⚠️ Must connect in series — NEVER across terminals
🔄
Clamp Ammeter
Non-intrusive clamp placed around a single conductor. No circuit interruption required during measurement.
✅ Safe and non-disruptive to the CP system
⚖️
Shunt Method
Measure voltage drop across a calibrated resistance (shunt). Apply Ohm's Law: I = V ÷ R to find the current.
📐 Precise — full-scale rating (e.g. 15 A / 50 mV)
⚠️
Safety Rule: A multimeter or ammeter set to current mode must never be connected in parallel (across voltage terminals). This creates a near-short-circuit through the meter and will blow the internal fuse or permanently damage the instrument.

Key Takeaways

  • 1
    Shunt Law: I = V ÷ R. The shunt resistance equals its full-scale voltage rating divided by its full-scale current rating (e.g. 50 mV ÷ 15 A = 0.00333 Ω).
  • 2
    2-Wire Method: Measures voltage drop across a pipe span with known resistance. Simple but relies on accurate span resistance data. Formula: I = Vspan ÷ Rspan.
  • 3
    4-Wire Method: Derives a calibration factor (A/mV) using a known injection current before measuring. Eliminates contact resistance errors — more accurate than 2-wire.
  • 4
    Current Direction: A positive voltage at lead A vs. lead B means conventional current flows from A toward B in the pipe. A negative voltage means the reverse. Direction identifies where CP current is entering or leaving the structure.

⚡ Electrical Continuity
All metallic components of a CP system must be electrically bonded — couplings, screw joints, expansion joints, flanges, rebar groups, and piling clusters. A break in continuity means CP current cannot reach that section.
Target: R ≈ 0 Ω (near-zero, fully bonded)
🚧 Electrical Isolation
Isolation fittings (insulating flanges, monolithic joints, swivel joints) deliberately break the electrical path to confine CP current to the protected structure and prevent current loss to adjacent metalwork.
Target: R = very high (no measurable current path)

⚡ Ohmmeter Error — Soil Parallel Path

When testing casing-to-pipe resistance, the surrounding soil forms a parallel electrical path. The ohmmeter reads the parallel combination of the true fitting resistance and the soil resistance — making a perfectly good fitting appear shorted.

Key Takeaways

  • 1
    Ohmmeter Limitation: Soil in the annulus provides a parallel path between the casing and carrier pipe. The measured resistance = Rtrue ∥ Rsoil. Wet soil can make a good fitting appear completely shorted.
  • 2
    Potential Difference Test: Measure pipe-to-soil potential on each side of the fitting. A difference > 100 mV confirms effective isolation. This avoids the ohmmeter's parallel-path error entirely.
  • 3
    Diode States: Normal → Fwd ≈ 0.3–0.9 V, Rev = OL. Short-circuit → both directions show ~0 V. Open-circuit → both directions show OL.
  • 4
    Continuity vs. Isolation: Bonds (near-zero R) allow CP current to reach all pipe sections. Isolation fittings (very high R) confine CP current to the protected zone. Both are essential for a well-designed CP system.

Soil Resistivity

Soil resistivity (ρ) is the most critical parameter in CP system design — it governs anode output, current distribution, and corrosion risk. The Wenner 4-pin method is the international standard for field measurement.

ρ = 2πaR Wenner 4-Pin Soil Layers Corrosion Risk

Measurement Methods

Most Common 📏
Wenner 4-Pin (ASTM G57)
Four equally spaced pins in a line. Outer pair (C1, C2) carries current; inner pair (P1, P2) measures potential drop. Effective depth ≈ pin spacing a.
ρ = 2π · a · R   (a in cm → ρ in Ω·cm)
🧪
Soil Box
Laboratory method. Soil sample packed in a calibrated box. Resistance measured and multiplied by box factor. Good for characterising core samples at different moisture levels.
🔩
2-Pin Method
Simpler setup with one current and one potential pin. Less accurate due to contact resistance contribution. Useful for quick screening surveys only.
📍
Single Probe
Single rod driven to depth; resistance measured to a remote earth. Gives localised resistivity at probe depth only — no depth profiling.
💡
Probe Placement Rule: Always position pins away from underground metallic structures (pipelines, rebar, cables). Metal provides a low-resistance parallel path that draws extra current and causes the measured resistivity to read falsely low.

Click any bar to see the typical ρ value and corrosion classification. NACE SP0169 classifies soils with ρ < 1 000 Ω·cm as extremely corrosive.

Key Takeaways

  • 1
    Wenner Formula: ρ = 2π · a · R, where a is pin spacing in cm and R is measured resistance in Ω. Result is in Ω·cm. Effective measurement depth ≈ pin spacing a.
  • 2
    Multiple Spacings: Vary pin spacing to profile different depths. Small a → top layer only. Large a → average of top + deep layers. A complete survey uses several spacings to build the full resistivity profile.
  • 3
    Corrosion Risk: Lower ρ = higher corrosion risk. ρ < 1 000 Ω·cm is extremely corrosive (wet clay, seawater); ρ > 30 000 Ω·cm is minimally corrosive (dry sand, rock). Water content and temperature both shift ρ significantly.
  • 4
    Avoid Metal Structures: Underground pipes, rebar, or cables near the array provide a low-resistance parallel current path, making ρ read falsely low. Always survey in clear ground away from known buried metalwork.

Special Equipment

Three categories of CP-specific instrumentation complete the field toolkit: pipe and cable locators that trace buried infrastructure, current interrupters that synchronise instant-off readings, and coupon test stations that provide accurate localised potential and current density data.

Disconnect Switch

Key Takeaways

  • 1
    Conductive vs Inductive: Conductive injects the signal directly onto the pipe — stronger signal, more accurate. Inductive couples EM energy through the air — no connection needed but weaker, more prone to cross-coupling to nearby metalwork.
  • 2
    Current Interrupter: Synchronises rectifier ON/OFF cycles across a pipeline system. Modern units use GPS timing. Typical cycle: 3–6 s ON, 1–2 s OFF. All operators read instant-off potential simultaneously.
  • 3
    Coupon Connected: Coupon sits at the same potential as the pipe (ON potential). Current through the lead wire = local CP current pickup at that point. A positive pickup means CP current is flowing into the coupon — protection is active.
  • 4
    Coupon Disconnected: Reads the instant-off (IR-free) potential — equivalent to opening the circuit at the pipe surface. Periodic retrieval and weighing of the coupon gives a direct corrosion rate measurement in mpy (mils per year).
